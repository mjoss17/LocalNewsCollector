<p><b>Bessemer</b> is a southwestern suburb of <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Alabama" title="Jefferson County, Alabama">Jefferson County, Alabama</a>, United States.<sup id="cite_ref-GNIS1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GNIS1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The population was 27,456 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 Census</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is within the Birmingham-<a href="/wiki/Hoover,_Alabama" title="Hoover, Alabama">Hoover</a>, AL <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_metropolitan_area,_Alabama" title="Birmingham metropolitan area, Alabama">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>, of which Jefferson County is the center. It developed rapidly as an industrial city in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. In 2019 it was named Alabama's "Worst City to Live in" by 24/7 Wall Street.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town was founded in the <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Reconstruction Era">postbellum era</a> by the Bessemer Land and Improvement Company, named after <a href="/wiki/Henry_Bessemer" title="Henry Bessemer">Henry Bessemer</a> and owned by coal magnate <a href="/wiki/Henry_F._DeBardeleben" title="Henry F. DeBardeleben">Henry F. DeBardeleben</a>. He had inherited <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pratt_(industrialist)" title="Daniel Pratt (industrialist)">Daniel Pratt</a>'s investments.<sup id="cite_ref-samfordmens_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samfordmens-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The mayor and councilmen voted to incorporate the city of Bessemer on September 9, 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Located 16 miles southwest of Birmingham, Bessemer grew rapidly and its promoters believed that it might overtake the other city in economic power.
</p><p>Given the iron ore, coal and limestone deposits in the area, the city became a center of steelmaking from about 1890 through the 20th century. It attracted rural migrants from across the South, as well as European immigrants. By the 1950s, the city was majority African American in population.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The industry went through considerable restructuring in the late 20th century, and jobs moved out of the area. Steel is no longer made there.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bessemer is located approximately 18 miles (29&#160;km) southwest of Birmingham.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 40.8 square miles (106&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), of which 40.7 square miles (105&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.1 square miles (0.26&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) (0.17%) is water.
</p><p>Bessemer is situated in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> district of Alabama, in the southern part of Jones Valley (about 3 miles (4.8&#160;km) wide).<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Iron ore was mined on the hills on the city's southeast side, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> was (and still is) mined to the north and west, and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> deposits were also nearby. All three ingredients were necessary for steelmaking, which led to the area becoming a major steel center from about 1890 through the twentieth century. Steel is no longer made within the city limits, but is still manufactured in the neighboring city of <a href="/wiki/Fairfield,_Alabama" title="Fairfield, Alabama">Fairfield</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate in this area is characterized by hot, humid summers and generally mild to cool winters.  According to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Bessemer has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a>, abbreviated "Cfa" on climate maps.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>As of the 2013 <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a>, there were 27,336 people living in the city. 72.0% were <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 24.0% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 0.1% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.2% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.1% from some other race and 0.4% from two or more races. 3.2% were <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.
</p><p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of 2000, there were 29,672 people, 11,537 households, and 7,868 families living in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 729.0 people per square mile (281.5/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 12,790 housing units at an average density of 314.2 per square mile (121.3/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 69.55% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 28.93% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>,  0.28% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.18% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.02% <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.30% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.74% from two or more races. 1.14% of the population were <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.
</p><p>There were 11,537 households, out of which 30.5% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 34.6% were married couples living together, 29.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 31.8% were non-families. 29.0% of all households were made up of individuals, and 13.0% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.52 and the average family size was 3.12.
</p><p>In the city, the population was spread out, with 26.8% under the age of 18, 9.6% from 18 to 24, 26.1% from 25 to 44, 21.1% from 45 to 64, and 16.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 36 years. For every 100 females, there were 82.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 75.8 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the city was $23,066, and the median income for a family was $28,230. Males had a median income of $29,413 versus $21,552 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $12,232. About 24.2% of families and 27.2% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 37.8% of those under age 18 and 24.7% of those age 65 or over.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bright_Star,_Bessemer,_Alabama.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg/170px-The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg/255px-The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg/340px-The_Bright_Star%2C_Bessemer%2C_Alabama.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="3648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bright_Star,_Bessemer,_Alabama.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Bright Star in Bessemer is Alabama's oldest restaurant<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg/220px-Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg/330px-Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg/440px-Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="680" data-file-height="352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amazon_groundbreaking_Bessemer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The groundbreaking of the Amazon fulfillment center in Bessemer in 2018.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1900, Bessemer ranked eighth in population in the state, second in amount of capital invested in manufacturing, and fourth in the value of its manufactured product for the year. By 1911, ore mining, iron smelting, and the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> were the chief industries of Bessemer.  Truck farming was also an important industry, dating from the area's agricultural past.
</p><p>Both blacks and whites from rural areas were attracted to the city for its new work opportunities. Gradually African Americans moved into industrial jobs and became part of integrated unions; such jobs enabled many working-class families to enjoy middle-class incomes.
</p><p>Today, ore mining has ended, as supplies were exhausted. Manufacturing remains a factor, with the U.S. Pipe and Foundry ductile pipe plant on the city's north side. On May 9, 2007, U.S. Pipe announced that it would be building a new $45-million foundry near the current plant.  The site was selected, among other reasons, for having available space for potential future expansions. U.S. Pipe is the largest domestic producer of Ductile Iron pipe in sizes 4 inch through 64 inch.
</p><p>The city was once home to a large railroad car manufacturing factory, operated by <a href="/wiki/Pullman_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Pullman Standard">Pullman Standard</a> for many decades and later by Trinity Industries. With railroad restructuring in the late 20th century and other manufacturing moving offshore, this plant ceased most production in the 1990s. Other industries have relocated to this facility.
</p><p>The decline of mining and exodus of the steelmaking and railcar manufacturing industries resulted in extensive loss of jobs. The city has lost population since a peak in 1970. It faced an economic crisis in the early to mid-1980s, as unemployed workers constituted more than one-third of the workforce. Since that time the city, through the efforts of the Bessemer Area Chamber of Commerce and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer_Industrial_Development_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bessemer Industrial Development Board (page does not exist)">Bessemer Industrial Development Board</a>, has been successful in diversifying its economy. It is recognized for its business growth.  In June 2018,  <a href="/wiki/Amazon_(company)" title="Amazon (company)">Amazon</a> announced that it would build a new 800,000 square feet (74,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>), $325 million fulfillment center in Bessemer which will initially create 1,500 new jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crime increased following the rise in unemployment and social disruption from the decline of manufacturing industries in the area. As of 2019 Bessemer ranks first in terms of violent crimes for US cities with 25,000 or more people.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bessemer uses the <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a>–<a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> form of government. The council has seven members, elected from <a href="/wiki/Single-member_districts" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-member districts">single-member districts</a>. As of 2016, Kenneth Gulley is mayor, a position elected <a href="/wiki/At-large" title="At-large">at-large</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He was first elected in 2010 and reelected to a second term in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A satellite <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Alabama" title="Jefferson County, Alabama">Jefferson County</a> <a href="/wiki/Courthouse" title="Courthouse">courthouse</a> is located in downtown Bessemer. There is a special county government district, known as the "Bessemer Cutoff", which was established in the middle of the 20th century when Bessemer was a major city in its own right. A separate county government was considered a possibility, but there was not sufficient land area to meet legislative requirements for a county. The "Cutoff" had a separate series of Alabama <a href="/wiki/License_plate" class="mw-redirect" title="License plate">license plates</a>, with a different numeric prefix than the rest of the county.
</p><p>Bessemer has since been surpassed in size by Birmingham suburbs such as <a href="/wiki/Hoover,_Alabama" title="Hoover, Alabama">Hoover</a>. But Bessemer retains the branch county courthouse to this day. The term "Bessemer Cutoff" continues to be used regularly by area residents.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> operates the Bessemer Post Office.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The state <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Department_of_Corrections" title="Alabama Department of Corrections">Alabama Department of Corrections</a> operates the <a href="/wiki/William_E._Donaldson_Correctional_Facility" title="William E. Donaldson Correctional Facility">William E. Donaldson Correctional Facility</a>, a prison for men, in <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Alabama" title="Jefferson County, Alabama">Jefferson County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, near Bessemer. The prison includes one of the two Alabama death rows for men.<sup id="cite_ref-DonaldProf_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DonaldProf-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_schools">Public schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bessemer operates its own school system independent of Jefferson County schools.  The system includes:
</p>
<ul><li>Hard Elementary</li>
<li>Jonesboro Elementary</li>
<li>Greenwood Elementary</li>
<li>Abrams Elementary</li>
<li>Westhills Elementary</li>
<li>Bessemer City Middle</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bessemer_City_High_School_(Alabama)" title="Bessemer City High School (Alabama)">Bessemer City High School</a> (formally Jess Lanier)</li></ul>
<p>The Board of Education also operates the Quitman Mitchell Opportunity Center, which includes an adult learning center, Even Start child care center, and New Horizon Alternative School.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_schools">Private schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Private schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>K–12 private schools in the Bessemer included Bessemer Academy and Rock Christian School.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_college">Community college</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Community college">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lawson_State_Community_College" title="Lawson State Community College">Lawson State Community College</a> operates the former Bessemer Technical College campus. The two schools merged in 2005 as a cost-saving measure.
</p>

<p><i>The Western Star</i> is a weekly newspaper which covers Bessemer and nearby communities.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Birmingham_News" title="The Birmingham News">The Birmingham News</a></i> is published three days per week, and also publishes a weekly section devoted to news from Bessemer and nearby communities.
</p><p>One <a href="/wiki/Radio_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Radio station">radio station</a>, <a href="/wiki/WZGX" title="WZGX">WZGX</a> (1450 AM), operates within the city; it broadcasts some Spanish programming and music to appeal to the growing Mexican-American population of Jefferson County. It also continues a tradition of broadcasting high school football games on Friday nights. All of metro Birmingham's stations are heard in Bessemer, as well as several stations broadcasting from <a href="/wiki/Tuscaloosa,_Alabama" title="Tuscaloosa, Alabama">Tuscaloosa</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">Television station</a> <a href="/wiki/WDBB" class="mw-redirect" title="WDBB">WDBB</a> (channel 17) is licensed to Bessemer, but broadcasts from studios in Birmingham, simulcasting with <a href="/wiki/WTTO" title="WTTO">WTTO</a> (channel 21). All of Birmingham's television stations may be viewed in Bessemer, and some have established news bureaus there.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bessemer,_Alabama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1911, the town was served by five <a href="/wiki/Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Railroad">railroad</a> lines: <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Great_Southern" class="mw-redirect" title="Alabama Great Southern">Alabama Great Southern</a> (Queen &amp; Crescent route), the <a href="/wiki/Louisville_%26_Nashville_Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Louisville &amp; Nashville Railroad">Louisville &amp; Nashville Railroad</a>, the Kansas City, Memphis &amp; Birmingham (<a href="/wiki/St._Louis_%26_San_Francisco_Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis &amp; San Francisco Railroad">St. Louis &amp; San Francisco Railroad</a> system), the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Southern_Railroad" title="Birmingham Southern Railroad">Birmingham Southern Railroad</a>, and the Atlanta, Birmingham &amp; Atlantic railways. Passenger service decreased after people started choosing to travel by automobiles, increasingly so after World War II. In addition, there was widespread restructuring in the railroad industry that also applied to freight lines.
</p><p>By 2006, the companies noted above had consolidated to <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a>, which has lines to Birmingham and <a href="/wiki/Brookwood,_Alabama" title="Brookwood, Alabama">Brookwood</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway" title="Norfolk Southern Railway">Norfolk Southern Railway</a>, with lines to Birmingham, <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>; Birmingham Southern continues in service. A major railroad feature is the "High Line", constructed by Tennessee Coal &amp; Iron (predecessor to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Steel" title="U.S. Steel">U.S. Steel</a>) to ship iron ore from the mines on the city's south side to the steel works in nearby <a href="/wiki/Fairfield,_Alabama" title="Fairfield, Alabama">Fairfield</a>. This elevated line traverses the eastern side of the city. Though tracks were removed over much of the High Line when the mines closed, part of the line is still used by the Birmingham Southern. All of the roadbed and bridges remain in place.
</p><p>Bessemer is served by the small <a href="/wiki/Bessemer_Airport" title="Bessemer Airport">Bessemer Airport</a> to the southeast of the city. Commercial service in the region is provided by the much larger <a href="/wiki/Birmingham-Shuttlesworth_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Birmingham-Shuttlesworth International Airport">Birmingham-Shuttlesworth International Airport</a>, located 5 miles (8.0&#160;km) north of downtown Birmingham (about 21 miles away in total).
</p><p>Major highways in Bessemer include <a href="/wiki/Interstate_20_(Alabama)" class="mw-redirect" title="Interstate 20 (Alabama)">I-20</a>/<a href="/wiki/Interstate_59_(Alabama)" class="mw-redirect" title="Interstate 59 (Alabama)">59</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_459" title="Interstate 459">I-459</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_11_in_Alabama" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Route 11 in Alabama">U.S. Route 11</a>, and <a href="/wiki/State_Route_150_(Alabama)" class="mw-redirect" title="State Route 150 (Alabama)">State Route 150</a>, which connects Bessemer with <a href="/wiki/Hoover,_Alabama" title="Hoover, Alabama">Hoover</a>.
</p>
